Resistor Color Code Overview

Through-hole resistors use a color code with bands to represent their resistance value. The first two (or three) bands are significant digits, the next band is the multiplier, and the last band indicates tolerance.

There can be three to six color bands on a resistor, and the meaning of these bands depends on the type of resistor. Below is the meaning of bands for each resistor type:

  • 3-band resistor: 2 significant digits, 1 multiplier
  • 4-band resistor: 2 significant digits, 1 multiplier, 1 tolerance
  • 5-band resistor: 3 significant digits, 1 multiplier, 1 tolerance
  • 6-band resistor: 3 significant digits, 1 multiplier, 1 tolerance, 1 temperature coefficient

Calculating Resistance

To calculate the resistance of a resistor with a given color code:

  1. Identify the color bands and their corresponding values.
  2. Use the formula: Resistance = (First Digit × 10 + Second Digit) × Multiplier.
  3. Apply the tolerance value to determine the acceptable range.

Example Calculation

For example, a 4-band resistor with colors Red, Black, Brown, Gold would be calculated as follows:

First Digit (Red)
2
Second Digit (Black)
0
Multiplier (Brown)
10
Tolerance (Gold)
±5%

Result: Resistance = (2 × 10 + 0) × 10 = 200 Ω ±5% (range: 190–210 Ω)
